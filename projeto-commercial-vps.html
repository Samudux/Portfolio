B2B STRATEGY RESEARCH

Commercial VPS

Applied engineering in the field

Context

Commercial VPS is a B2B industrial company with 25 years in the market, specializing in industrial torque tools and applied engineering solutions. The project was a complete repositioning of their digital presence — not a visual refresh, but a structural rethinking of how the company communicates value to technical buyers.

Problem

VPS had 25 years of technical expertise and proven field results — but their digital presence communicated none of it. The site looked like every other industrial distributor: product grids, spec sheets, and a generic contact form. Visitors couldn't tell why they should work with VPS instead of any other supplier.

Solution

Reposition the site as a consultative, diagnosis-first platform. Instead of presenting products upfront, the system qualifies the visitor's context — industry, challenge type, urgency — and routes them to the right specialist before any catalog appears. The message becomes: "we solve your problem, and the product is how we do it."

Research

Before any design decision, I needed to understand the competitive landscape and who was actually visiting this site — and what they needed when they arrived.

Competitive analysis

Five direct competitors were analyzed: Carlsons, Durazzo, Enerpac, HYTORC and Atlas Copco. The pattern was consistent — all of them led with product catalogs and spec sheets. None of them led with the client's problem. This was the gap: a consultative positioning was not just a differentiator — it was entirely absent from the market.

Two visitor profiles

An empathy map revealed two distinct B2B visitor types: the Technical in Search of Direction — who knows they have a problem but doesn't know what solution exists — and the Objective Technical Buyer — who already knows what they need and is evaluating suppliers. The existing site failed both: it didn't diagnose the first and didn't differentiate for the second.

Strategic Decision — The Turning Point

The key insight from research changed the entire direction of the project.

Industrial B2B buyers don't visit a site to browse. They visit with a problem — and their first question is not "what do you sell?" but "can you solve what I have?" The moment a site leads with a product catalog, it loses the visitor who doesn't yet know what product they need.

This shifted the design question from "how do we present our products better?" to "how do we qualify the visitor's context before showing them anything?"

Three strategic principles were defined from this insight:

Solution before catalog

The visitor's problem is the entry point — not the product. Every structural decision in the site follows this hierarchy.

Expert in applied engineering

VPS doesn't just distribute tools — they apply them in context. The site needed to communicate this distinction clearly and consistently.

Visible operational impact

Real case studies in the field — not just product descriptions. The evidence of expertise must be visible before the sale happens.

Product Decisions

Industry-first navigation

The previous structure organized content by product category. Technical buyers think in terms of their industry and operational challenge — not in terms of tool families. Navigation was restructured around industry segments and challenge types, mapping directly to how the visitor thinks.

AI Technical Triage

A hypothesis was developed for a lightweight triage assistant: three structured questions about industry, challenge and urgency, routing the visitor to the right specialist before any catalog interaction. The goal was to qualify the conversation, not automate the sale — the specialist closes the deal; the system gets the right person to them.

Field cases as primary evidence

Case studies were elevated from a secondary section to a core component of the value proposition. Operational impact — real numbers, real contexts — communicates expertise more effectively than any product description.

CTA before catalog

In the previous structure, contact happened after catalog browsing. For the consultative model, the CTA moves earlier in the journey: "request a technical evaluation" replaces "see our products." The conversion event is a qualified conversation — not a page view.

Trade-offs

Consultative depth vs. SEO discoverability

Catalog-heavy sites tend to rank better for product-specific searches. The consultative approach sacrifices some catalog surface area in favor of positioning clarity. The bet is that qualified leads are worth more than high-volume undifferentiated traffic.

Technical language vs. accessibility

The primary audience is technical — but not always equally specialized. Using highly technical language strengthens credibility with specialists but creates friction for procurement contacts and decision-makers who aren't engineers. The solution was to layer: clear summary language on the surface, technical depth one level down.

Triage assistance vs. direct access

The Objective Technical Buyer already knows what they need — routing them through a triage flow creates unnecessary friction. The triage assistant was designed as an optional layer, not a gate. Direct access to products and specialists was preserved as a parallel path.

Interface

Hero & Value Proposition

Consultative positioning upfront: the message leads with the client's problem, not the product catalog.

Industries & Technical Assistant

Navigation organized by industry segment, with a 3-question triage routing visitors to the right specialist before any catalog.

Field Cases & CTA

Real operational results in context, with a direct call to a technical evaluation — result before catalog.

Learnings

The most significant shift in this project was understanding what conversion actually means in B2B. The site's job is not to close the sale — it's to qualify the conversation. Getting the right person in the room with the right specialist is the actual conversion event. Everything else is noise reduction.

Research also revealed something that changed the design direction completely: when you analyze what competitors are doing and find they all do the same thing, that's not best practice — that's a positioning gap. VPS's differentiation didn't come from better visual design. It came from being the only one willing to lead with the client's problem.
